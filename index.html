Finding The Relationship Between Emission and GDP By Countries¶

By: Ruijie Chen¶


Introduction¶

At a time when sustainable development has become a global imperative, understanding the complex interplay between economic growth and environmental impact is critical. Economic growth, often quantified as gross domestic product (GDP), drives many national policies. However, this growth is often accompanied by an increase in environmental costs, primarily in greenhouse gas emissions, which are a significant cause of global warming and climate.
For more information about GDP and Emission , Click on the corresponnding links.

In this tutorial, we aim to reveal and analyze the relationship between GDP and emissions across countries using powerful data science methods. By exploring this relationship, we aim to gain insight into how economic activity is linked to environmental impacts and the extent to which rich economies contribute to greater environmental degradation or to achieving green, sustainable growth.

We will leverage a comprehensive data science pipeline, starting with the collection and management of reliable data from international databases. We will process and clean this data to ensure the accuracy of our findings. We will use statistical tools and visualization techniques to analyze the data to determine the nature of the correlation between GDP and emissions. This investigation will help us understand whether higher GDP is always associated with higher emissions, or whether countries can decouple economic growth from environmental damage.

Through research, we will find ways for different countries to strike a balance between prosperity and sustainability, and thus better understand how the world should contribute to climate change.

Next, we need to prepare several data for our next Section:
For instance, mean(), Standard Deviation(STD), normalize the table.
We need to normalize the data because of the next few reasons:

  • As we observed, the scale of GDP is much larger than the scale of emissions. Normalizing these values would prevent the GDP from overpowering tihe influence of emissions in machine learning models.
  • Normalization allows each variable to contribute equally to the analysis, ensuring that insights derived from the data are not biased.
  • Having features on a similar scale can help improve the convergence models.>

We we Normalize the data using the function below.

In [192]:
columns_to_normalize = ['Value', 'Total', 'Coal', 'Oil', 'Gas', 'Cement', 'Flaring', 'Other']
def normalize(name,data):
    for n in name:
        current = data[n]
        current_mean = current.mean()
        current_std = current.std()
        current = (current - current_mean)/current_std
        data[n] = current
    return data

X_rest = Rest_70[['Year', 'Value']]
y_rest = Rest_70['Total']

X_rest_train, X_rest_test, y_rest_train, y_rest_test = train_test_split(X_rest, y_rest, test_size=0.1, random_state=50)

model_rest = LinearRegression()
model_rest.fit(X_rest_train, y_rest_train)

y_Top30_pred = model_Top30.predict(X_Top30_test)
y_rest_pred = model_rest.predict(X_rest_test)

MSE = (mse_Top30 * len(y_Top30_test) + mse_rest * len(y_rest_test)) / (len(y_Top30_test) + len(y_rest_test))
